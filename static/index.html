How It All Works · كيف يعمل النظام

The full pipeline end-to-end — and how the trained models travel from the research PC into this website. · المسار الكامل، وكيف تنتقل النماذج المدرَّبة من حاسوب الباحثة إلى هذا الموقع.

Part 1 — The pipeline, step by step

  1. Identify the sender by their iris — the biometric replaces a username.
  2. Vet the message — is it authentic or fake? (English or Arabic classifier.)
  3. Hide the message inside an ordinary image so no one suspects it exists (steganography).
  4. Encrypt the image using the biometric as the key — faithful RC4 or secure AES-GCM.
  5. Transmit the scrambled package to the receiver.
  6. Receiver reverses everything — decrypts, extracts the message, and verifies it arrived intact.

  5. إرسال الحزمة المشفّرة إلى المُستقبِل.

Part 2 — How the data & models get into the website

The key idea: the raw datasets never come to the website. Only the trained result does.

  1. Training happens once, on the research PC. The models read all 3.9 GB of data — the MMU iris images (152 MB), 134,000 English news rows (27 MB), and the Arabic dataset — and learn the patterns. This heavy computation is done locally.
  2. The result is distilled into tiny model files (~12 MB total): iris.joblib (5.7 MB), text_en.joblib (4.2 MB), text_ar.joblib (1.1 MB). They hold what was learned, not the data itself.
  3. Those files are imported into the website exactly as they are — committed into the project and deployed to the server. When you upload one iris or type one message, the site loads these ready-made models and answers instantly. It never needs the original datasets again.
  4. Why this design: the website stays tiny (12 MB, not 3.9 GB), loads fast, runs on a free server, and the private datasets stay on the PC — never published online.
  5. To update: if the datasets improve, re-run train_models.py on the PC to regenerate the 12 MB of models, push them, and the site redeploys automatically with the new models.

Think of a graduate who studied thousands of textbooks: the textbooks (data) stay home — only the knowledge (12 MB models) comes to work.

Sender Identification

Iris biometric recognition — establishes who is transmitting.

0

Iris Recognition

Fisherfaces model (PCA → LDA) over the MMU Iris Database — the scikit-learn analogue of the original FisherFace recognizer (modeliris.yml / rtsig.cpp).

What's happening in this step?
What it does

You upload a picture of someone's iris (the coloured ring of the eye), and the computer guesses which person it belongs to — like face recognition, but for eyes.

Why it matters for the research

In this system the sender is identified by their own body — their iris — instead of a username. That identity is what ties the person to their secret encryption key. This is the "who is sending?" part of the thesis.

How it learned to do this

A model was trained once on the MMU iris database (hundreds of eye photos from 45 people). It uses the classic Fisherfaces method — the same idea as the original FisherFace recognizer in the desktop program.

How to read the result

It shows the predicted person, a confidence %, and its top-3 guesses. It is right about 62% of the time (out of 45 people — random guessing would be ~2%), so it sometimes misses; the confidence number tells you how sure it is.

§1

Transmitter Protocol

Cover acquisition → preprocessing → feature audit → vetting → embedding → encryption → dispatch.

I

Cover Acquisition

Select a cover image. Its pixel field becomes the carrier for the hidden payload.

What's happening in this step?
What it does

You choose an ordinary image. This is the "cover" — the innocent-looking picture that will secretly carry the hidden message inside it.

Why it matters for the research

Steganography is about hiding a message so well that no one even suspects one exists. The cover image looks completely normal to anyone who sees it — the secret is buried in the pixels.

How to read the result

It shows the image size and its capacity — the maximum number of characters that can be hidden inside this particular image. Bigger images can hold longer messages.

II

Preprocessing

Grayscale conversion and histogram equalization, as in the C++ recognizer front-end (rtsig.cpp).

What's happening in this step?
What it does

It converts the image to black-and-white (grayscale) and then evens out its brightness (histogram equalization) so dark and bright areas are more balanced.

Why it matters for the research

This is exactly what the original C++ program (rtsig.cpp) did before recognising an iris. Standardising every image the same way makes the later analysis fair and repeatable.

How to read the result

The two histograms are bar charts showing how many pixels are dark vs. bright. After equalization the bars spread out more evenly. Entropy measures how much visual "information" the image holds (higher = more detailed).

III

Local-Feature Audit

Scale-invariant keypoint extraction over the cover (the desktop prototype's SURF stage).

What's happening in this step?
What it does

It finds the most distinctive points in the image — corners, edges, textured spots — and marks them with little circles. These are called keypoints.

Why it matters for the research

The original program used a method called SURF to describe an image by its key points. (SURF is patented and unavailable here, so the lab uses SIFT — its close, well-known cousin — and tells you which one ran.)

How to read the result

You'll see the image covered in circles and a count of how many keypoints were found. More keypoints means the image is richer in unique detail.

IV

LSB Embedding

The secret message is written bit-by-bit into pixel least-significant bits: a 4-byte length header, then the payload — 8 carrier bytes per hidden byte.

What's happening in this step?
First — vet the message (optional)

Before hiding, you can ask the computer whether the message looks authentic or fake. A model trained on real vs. fake news (in English or Arabic) gives its verdict. This is the "is the message trustworthy?" part of the thesis.

Then — hide the message

Your text is turned into 0s and 1s and tucked into the least important bit of each pixel's colour value. Changing that last bit shifts a colour so slightly the human eye cannot see any difference — but the message is now inside the image.

How to read the result

PSNR and MSE measure how different the new image is from the original. A high PSNR (70+ dB) means the change is essentially invisible — which is exactly what good steganography wants. It also shows how many pixels were touched.

V

Encryption & Dispatch

The stego image is encrypted under a biometric feature-vector key; the key itself is AES-wrapped before transmission. Choose the faithful RC4 port or the modernized authenticated mode.

What's happening in this step?
What it does

It scrambles the whole image so it becomes meaningless noise to anyone who intercepts it. The scrambling key comes from an iris feature vector — numbers derived from a person's eye — so the biometric is the key. That key is then locked inside another layer (AES) for transport.

The two modes (why there's a choice)

RC4 · faithful reproduces the original thesis exactly, so results stay comparable — but RC4 is old and no longer secure. AES-256-GCM · secure is the modern upgrade: it also detects tampering and derives a strong key properly (PBKDF2). Use RC4 to match the old numbers; use AES-GCM to show the improved, defensible version.

How to read the result

The scrambled image is shown as random noise, and its histogram should look flat. Entropy near 8.0 means the output is almost perfectly random — the sign of strong encryption.

§2

Receiver Protocol

Unwrap key → decrypt (RC4 or authenticated AES-GCM) → LSB extraction → integrity verdict.

VI

Channel Inbox

Packages arriving over the simulated two-socket channel (key : 5555 · payload : 4000).

What's happening in this step?
What it does

This is the receiver's side. It takes the scrambled package that was sent, reverses every step — unlocks the key, un-scrambles the image, and pulls the hidden message back out.

Why it matters for the research

It proves the whole journey works end-to-end: the secret that was hidden and encrypted on one side comes back out perfectly on the other. In the real thesis this happened between two computers over a network; here it's simulated in one place.

How to read the result

It shows the recovered message and a round-trip verdict: PASS means the message came back identical to what was sent (success!). With the secure mode it also confirms the package was not tampered with.
